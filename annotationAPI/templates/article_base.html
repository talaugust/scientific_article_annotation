Instructions

In this task, you will be asked to do the following:

  1. Read a news article about a medical or scientific finding.
  2. Highlight sections of the news article and answer questions about the article.
  3. Answer a set of demographic questions.

We expect parts 1-3 (required) to take approx. 10 minutes to complete total.

⛔ Participation restriction: providers/turkers for this task cannot currently be an employee or student at the University of Washington.

Thank you!

Additional study details Expand/collapse
Research motivation

We are studying what people pick up on when reading news about scientific and medical findings.

Data collection & sharing

We will not ask you for your name, and the data collected in this study will be made unidentifiable to the best of our ability. We will securely store the data on our servers and only share with qualified researchers. If you later decide that you do not want your responses included in this study, please email so we can exclude your work.

If you have questions about your rights as a research participant, or wish to obtain information, ask questions or discuss any concerns about this study with someone other than the researcher(s), please contact the University of Washington Human Subjects Division at 206-543-0098 (for international calls include the US Calling Code: +1-206-543-0098).

FAQ

  • Do I need to be a doctor or scientist to do this HIT?

    No! People of all backgrounds are welcome to perform this study. Unlike many annotation tasks, we are not looking for the "correct" answer, just a good faith effort at reading and answering questions about the text.

  • How should I label the annotations?

    The sections below will instruct you on how to label the annotations.

  • What demographic information will be collected?

    Age, gender, native language, and background/engagement in STEM subjects (science, technology, engineering, and math). We won't collect any personally identifying information (like your name).

  • Will any of my responses be saved before I submit the HIT?

    Your annotations will be saved and updated when you highlight sections of the article. As a result, please make sure you only highlight when asked to, and that all highlights you make are finalized when you hit the submit button.

  • Can I do this HIT multiple times?

    For now, no, though this study may inform related HITs in the future.

  • What if I have additional questions?

    Please reach out to Tal August (taugust@cs.washington.edu). Thanks!

Highlighting the lead

If there is a lead, please highlight the sentences that make up the lead.

Some articles have a few sentences at the beginning of the article, called a lead, that draws a reader in and makes them want to read more. Some examples of leads are below. If there is a lead in the first 1-2 sentences of the article, please highlight the lead sentence(s).

“On Wednesday, astronomers released what they said were the most detailed images ever taken of the surface of our sun.”

“SYDNEY, Australia — Early on the morning of Dec. 27, Phoebe Meagher, a wildlife conservation officer at Taronga Zoo, set off on a rescue mission with colleagues from the zoo and academics from the University of New South Wales.”

Main point(s) of the article

Please highlight all sentences in article that state the main findings of the article.

Usually a scientific news articles reports the main findings or point of the article. This includes the main findings being reported or a summary of what the article talks about. [Usually these sentences are found near the beginning of the article. ] Some examples are below.

“The study, by researchers at Tufts University and the International Union for Conservation of Nature, warned that fireflies could eventually face extinction globally because of multiple threats, including light pollution and habitat loss and habitat degradation from insecticides and chemical pollution.”

“In their study, published in the journal Science Advances, Yang and her colleagues at Georgia State and Emory University describe a newly identified biomarker for detection of liver metastases.”

Please label the all sentences that state the main findings with the label 'MAIN'.

'Why should I care?' sentences

Please highlight sentences in the article that talk about the real world impact of the science or findings being reported.

Scientific news articles often talk about the real world impact of the science or findings being reported. This can include future technologies or breakthroughs the findings might enable, or the societal implications of the findings. These sentences can occur anywhere in the article. Some examples are below:

““These ancient seeds might represent lost genetic diversity we don’t see any more,” Pérez-Escobar says. As date-palm growers adapt to climate change and battle pests and diseases, they might want to tap into the pool of ancient genes hidden in archaeological archives.”

“Insects like fireflies tend to be critical to their ecosystems. Their disappearance could create havoc with food webs, especially for the birds and other animals that feed on them.”

Please label the all sentences that state real world impact with the label 'IMPACT'.
